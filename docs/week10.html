Work / Week 10: Games & Play

A game that makes AI interviews feel unsettlingly real

An interactive narrative game exploring the gap between authentic self and performed identity in AI-driven evaluation systems.

The Artifact

→ Play The Interview Game

Link preview

The game opens a playable Twine narrative where AI evaluation, corporate language, and personal identity collide.

The Interview is an interactive narrative game built with Twine. It puts you in a waiting room where an AI interviewer, HK AI, evaluates your responses to real interview questions. The game has 9 passages with branching paths that converge toward two distinct endings.

The writing uses procedural rhetoric—mechanics like timed pauses (measured in decimal seconds), percentile comparisons, and data retention language make players feel the surveilling nature of the system itself.

Try the game: The best way to understand this project is to play it. It will take you about 5-10 minutes.

Design Statement

The game is about the gap between who you are and who you're expected to perform being—and how almost unbearable that gap becomes when the person on the other side of the desk isn't a person at all.

Every question that HK AI asks is technically fair:

  • "Describe yourself in three words."
  • "Describe a time you failed."
  • "If I told you this question didn't matter, would you still answer honestly?"

These are real interview questions. What the game does is make visible how strange they actually are when you can't rely on human intuition to read between the lines.

Key Mechanics

The choices that matter most are the ones that seem small. Answer honestly and get: "your answer deviates from the 94th percentile of successful candidates." Answer strategically and get: "alignment noted."

Neither response tells you whether you succeeded. The system acknowledges your input and processes it—you're left holding the feeling without knowing what it means.

The AI's timed pauses and the data retention revelation function as procedural rhetoric: you are not just being evaluated, you are being used.

The Two Endings

Ending A: You said something true and that happened regardless of the outcome.

Ending B: You named the game, and the game continued anyway.

Neither ending is a win. They're just two honest ways to leave a room that was never designed for you.

Artist Statement

I came into this thinking games were fundamentally about freedom. I came out understanding that games are about constraint, and what constraint reveals.

The Interview doesn't give you freedom. It gives you choices that feel meaningful and then quietly reminds you that the system is processing them regardless of your framing. That's not a bug. That's the argument.

AI Collaboration & Use

What Claude helped with: Structural thinking about emotional arc, brainstorming AI interviewer questions, and technical assistance with Twine's syntax.

What I changed: Claude's suggestions tended toward explicit; I preferred ambiguity. Every suggested passage was substantially rewritten. The two endings were entirely my own.

My policy: I use AI as a thinking partner, not a ghostwriter. Every sentence in this game reflects my voice and editorial decisions.
