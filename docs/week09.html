Week 9: Bots & Generators

A Tracery-based generative system creating surreal product descriptions. Explores curation as authorship.

The Artifact

I built a text generator using Tracery that creates surreal product listings—fake descriptions for objects that deal in emotion, memory, and dread. The generator has no practical function. It exists to generate weird, emotionally coherent text.

Through multiple iterations, I curated 7 standout outputs. Here are three examples:

"The Unnamed Thing." Made from compressed forgetting, translucent like an apology. A sphere that is not quite touching itself. It locates the feeling you misplaced. Extracted from a recurring dream with permission. Feels like holding warm sand that is also listening. Side effects may include an unfamiliar smell in the left nostril. Free, but not without consequence. Warning: Keep away from unfinished conversations.
"The Pale Mirror." A pale grey that hums, flat in a way that feels personal. Sourced from the space between Wednesday and Thursday. Primary use: absorbs ambient regret. It feels like pressing your palm against a fogged window from the inside. Known side effects: a sudden memory of a house you have never visited.
"Found at the bottom of a sound: The Quiet Instrument." Constructed entirely of compressed forgetting. Appears translucent like an apology. Like a door that forgot what it was for. Designed to locate the feeling you misplaced. Side effects may include the sensation of being briefly watched. My reflection started using it too.

Process Notes

I built the generator using Tracery, a grammar-based text generation tool accessible at tracery.io. The generator contains 12 symbols (product_name, material, color, shape, function, etc.) with 5-8 options each, written toward a specific emotional register: unsettling and dreamy.

I generated approximately 60 outputs total by repeatedly clicking regenerate. Many were coherent but unremarkable. I stopped when I felt I had enough variety to curate meaningfully, settling on 7 final selections from the pool.

What surprised me: novelty exhausted itself quickly. After ~20 outputs, I stopped asking "is this interesting?" and started asking "is this the most interesting version of this idea?" That shift from generation to judgment felt significant. The question became about curation, not production.

Reflection

Where is the human in generative work? I think the honest answer is: everywhere except the middle. I defined the inputs (the grammar), I selected the outputs (the curation), and everything in between was a process I set in motion but did not control.

The outputs I chose share something rejected ones didn't: internal coherence. Each random combination somehow confirms the others. That coherence was never programmed. It was noticed. And noticing, I think, is still a human act.

This project taught me that curation is its own kind of authorship—slower, more uncomfortable, and more revealing than generation. The system doesn't have taste, but it inherits mine. The real creativity happens in deciding what to keep, what to throw away, and why.

Attribution & AI Use

Tools Used: Tracery (a free grammar-based text generation tool)

Process: All grammar rules, word lists, and sentence templates were written by me and entered into the Tracery editor as a JSON file. No AI writing tools were used to generate outputs. All curated outputs are direct products of my grammar, selected and annotated by me.

My Policy on AI Use: I use AI as a thinking partner, not a ghostwriter. The voice in these descriptions is mine. The system amplifies my sensibility, but doesn't replace it.
