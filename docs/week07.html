Week 7: Mapping AI Worlds

A critical counter-map of Google and Meta's global infrastructure, revealing the hidden geography of data centers, content moderation labor, and the externalized costs of AI systems.

The Artifact

Interactive Map: View the Google My Maps project →

This map visualizes the planetary infrastructure of Google and Meta: data centers, corporate headquarters, and content moderation facilities. By placing labor and hardware in the same visual field, the map argues that AI infrastructure is neither virtual nor abstract—it is deeply material and deeply unequal.

Process Notes

This research drew on three tiers of sources: official company documents (SEC filings, investor relations), independent trackers and technical analyses, and investigative journalism.

Sources Used

  • Corporate headquarters: Alphabet and Meta official investor relations and SEC filings
  • Data centers: Company infrastructure pages, independent trackers (Baxtel, dgtlinfra), and Data Center Dynamics
  • Content moderation labor: TIME Magazine investigations, CNN Business reporting, Bureau of Investigative Journalism, and Foxglove
  • Energy & environmental impact: Entergy Louisiana press releases, Inside Climate News

What Was Hardest to Find

The content moderation layer was the most difficult to research. Neither Google nor Meta publishes a list of their outsourcing vendors, facility addresses, or contractor wages. This opacity is structural, not accidental. The outsourcing model—where companies contract BPO firms who contract subcontractors who hire workers—is designed to diffuse accountability across layers. The missing data itself is the argument.

What Surprised Me

The geographic logic of harm concentration is striking: data centers locate where energy is cheapest (rural Iowa, Arctic Sweden). Content moderation labor locates where wages are lowest and organized labor is weakest (Philippines, Kenya, Ghana). The pattern is not random—it follows and reinforces existing global inequalities.

I also underestimated the human cost at the data center layer. Google's Oregon facility consumes 29% of a small city's water supply. Meta's Louisiana campus will require three new natural gas plants. The "clean AI" narrative collapses when mapped against physical infrastructure.

Reflection

This map makes one central argument: AI is not virtual. It is a system of physical extraction whose costs are distributed globally while profits pool in a fifteen-mile radius of the San Francisco Peninsula.

If I had mapped only data centers, I would have reproduced what Meta already publishes—polished maps with renewable energy statistics. My map refuses that frame. Placing moderation labor alongside hardware insists that the human cost of making AI "safe" be counted alongside the machine cost of running it.

D'Ignazio and Klein argue in Data Feminism that power operates through what is systematically obscured, not only through what is shown. This counter-map attempts to reverse that logic. What remains missing—individual worker names, wages, NDAs—has never been collected because collecting it would force accountability. The missing data is the argument.

Attribution & AI Use

Claude (Anthropic) was used in the research phase to assist with organizing data points, compiling facts about data center locations and labor conditions, and drafting research documentation and artist statements based on my direction and source materials. All factual claims were verified against primary and investigative sources. Final editorial decisions, map design, and argumentative framing are my own.
