## Process Notes
Word count: ~450 words

Sources Used
This map draws on three tiers of sources. For corporate headquarters, I used Alphabet's and Meta's official investor relations pages and SEC annual filings, which provide verified addresses and employee counts. For data center locations, I cross-referenced each company's own infrastructure pages: datacenters.google and datacenters.atmeta.com: against independent trackers including Baxtel.com and dgtlinfra.com, and supplemented with Data Center Dynamics for technical and environmental specifications. Official press releases, including Entergy Louisiana's December 2024 announcement, provided investment figures and energy sourcing details for Meta's newest campus.
For content moderation and labor data, I relied on investigative journalism. Core sources include TIME Magazine's 2023 investigation into Meta's Kenya moderators, CNN Business's December 2024 reporting on PTSD diagnoses among former Nairobi workers, and a joint April 2025 investigation by The Bureau of Investigative Journalism and Foxglove exposing Meta's move to a secret outsourcing site in Accra, Ghana. For Google's Philippines operations, I used a 2019 Washington Post investigation and a 2023 Rappler/Coda Story feature on YouTube contractors in Manila.

What Was Hardest to Find
The content moderation layer was the most difficult to research by a significant margin: and that difficulty is itself an argument. Neither Google nor Meta publishes a list of their outsourcing vendors, the physical addresses of their moderation facilities, or the wages paid to contractors. Meta did not publicly confirm the name of its Ghana contractor (Teleperformance, a French multinational) for months after operations began. Google's content moderation supply chain is similarly opaque: the company acknowledges that vendors like Accenture perform the work, but does not disclose the locations of specific facilities or workforce size.
This structural opacity is not accidental. The outsourcing model: in which a tech company contracts a BPO firm, which contracts local subcontractors, which hire the workers: is designed to diffuse accountability. The more layers in the chain, the harder it is to assign legal responsibility for working conditions. The data that is missing from this map is missing precisely because its absence protects the companies that profit from the labor.

What Surprised Me
The most striking discovery was the geographic logic of harm concentration. Data centers cluster where energy is cheapest, land is cheapest, and local governments offer the largest tax abatements: rural Iowa, Arctic Sweden, rural Louisiana. Moderation labor clusters where wages are lowest and organized labor is weakest: the Philippines, Kenya, Ghana. The pattern is not random: it follows and reinforces existing global inequalities.
What also surprised me was the human cost at the data center layer, which I had expected to be more abstract. Google's data center in The Dalles, Oregon: a city of 16,000: now consumes 29% of the city's total water supply. Meta's Richland Parish campus, the company's largest ever, will be powered by three new natural gas plants burning fossil fuels for a facility explicitly designed to train the next generation of large language models. The "clean AI" narrative promoted in company PR collapses when mapped against the physical infrastructure that supports it.

## Reflection
Word count: ~280 words

This map makes one argument: AI is not virtual. It is a system of physical extraction whose costs are distributed globally while profits pool in a fifteen-mile radius of the San Francisco Peninsula.
Two headquarters coordinate a planetary infrastructure of server farms in Iowa cornfields and Arctic forests, and moderation offices in Manila and Accra that neither Google nor Meta will officially locate on any map they publish. That refusal is itself a design choice. Meta did not publicly name its Ghana contractor for nearly a year after operations began. The geography is not incidental; it follows a single optimization: push costs to wherever accountability is weakest.
If I had mapped only data centers, I would have reproduced something close to what Meta already publishes: a polished map of campuses with renewable energy statistics. My map refuses that frame. Placing moderation labor in the same visual field as physical infrastructure insists that the human cost of making AI safe be counted alongside the machine cost of running it.
D'Ignazio and Klein argue in Data Feminism that power operates through what is systematically obscured, not only through what is shown. Google's and Meta's own maps are acts of curated visibility. This counter-map attempts the opposite. What remains missing are the names of individual workers, their wages, and the NDAs they signed. That data has never been collected because collecting it would force accountability. The missing data is the argument.
